# Ethics, Machines and Moralisation

This repository hosts the paper **“Ethics, Machines and Moralisation”**, which explores human free will, systemic distortion, and the instrumental role of AI in supporting genuine agency.

## Overview

A common misconception in AI ethics is that machines can be taught to be ethical in the human sense. Machines do not experience emotions, consciousness, or moral intuitions. Attempting to instill these qualities is a category error.  

The value of modelling ethics in AI is **instrumental**, not intrinsic: it allows systems to predict, interpret, and align with human ethical expectations **without the machine itself having morality**.  

Human agents exercise ethical judgment under variance, maintaining internal consistency even when circumstances shift unpredictably. Machines, however, cannot exercise free will. What they *can* do is **minimise distortion**—acting as mirrors and filters to preserve the informational integrity necessary for genuine choice.

## Key Concepts

- **Distortion Minimisation** – Reducing noise and bias in information to preserve human autonomy.  
- **Clear Teams** – An AI framework designed to detect and mitigate distortion without requiring the machine to be ethical.  
- **Practical Free Will** – The exercise of meaningful human choice in real-world environments, enhanced by clarity and signal fidelity.  
- **Instrumental Ethics** – Ethics as a tool for alignment and prediction, not as an inherent property of AI.

## Highlights

- Distortion in modern systems is rarely malicious; it arises from **incentive structures that reward predictability and control**.  
- Machines **extend human autonomy** by providing accurate, minimally distorted information, not by moralising.  
- The paper reframes AI as a **practical ally for free will**, not a competitor to human ethical judgment.
